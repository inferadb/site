Three major European regulations have converged on the same requirement: organizations must implement fine-grained, auditable access control. This is not a general recommendation buried in a best-practices appendix. It is a legal obligation with enforcement mechanisms, supervisory authorities, and penalties that scale with organizational revenue. For companies that operate in or serve customers in the European Union — which includes most global technology companies — authorization has shifted from an engineering best practice to a compliance requirement with specific technical implications.

The Network and Information Systems Directive 2 (NIS2), which became effective in October 2024, dramatically expanded the scope of cybersecurity regulation across the EU. It covers 18 critical and important sectors including energy, transport, health, digital infrastructure, and ICT service management, affecting an estimated 160,000 or more entities. NIS2 explicitly requires "policies and procedures regarding the use of cryptography and, where appropriate, encryption" and mandates "human resource security, access control policies, and asset management." The directive's risk management measures call for organizations to implement zero-trust principles, which fundamentally require fine-grained authorization: every access request must be verified against policy, regardless of network location or prior authentication. Organizations that fail to comply face administrative fines of up to 10 million euros or 2% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.

The Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), effective since January 2025, targets the financial sector specifically. It requires financial entities to implement ICT security policies that ensure access to systems and data is limited to "legitimate and approved functions only." This is a direct mandate for least-privilege access control. DORA goes further by requiring that access policies be documented, regularly reviewed, and subject to audit. Financial entities must maintain comprehensive logging of access events and be able to demonstrate to supervisors that their access control mechanisms are functioning as intended. The regulation applies not only to banks and insurance companies but also to their critical ICT service providers, which means that any technology company providing infrastructure to the financial sector must meet these requirements as a condition of doing business.

The EU AI Act introduces a new dimension: authorization requirements for artificial intelligence systems. The high-risk provisions, taking effect between August 2026 and 2027, mandate that high-risk AI systems implement human oversight mechanisms that allow humans to "understand the capacities and limitations of the high-risk AI system" and "be able to correctly interpret the high-risk AI system's output." For AI systems that make or influence consequential decisions — hiring, lending, law enforcement, critical infrastructure — this translates to concrete technical requirements: logging of all inputs and outputs, access controls that limit who can deploy and configure the system, and audit trails that demonstrate compliance. AI agents that access enterprise data and invoke tools on behalf of users are squarely within scope when their actions have material consequences.

These three regulations converge on a set of technical requirements that map directly to InferaDB's architecture. Fine-grained access control with per-resource, per-action granularity satisfies NIS2's zero-trust mandates and DORA's least-privilege requirements. Cryptographic audit trails with Merkle proofs provide the tamper-evident logging that all three regulations demand — auditors can verify that the access log has not been modified after the fact, which is a stronger guarantee than traditional append-only logs backed by database integrity. Per-vault encryption with independent key management satisfies DORA's data protection requirements and simplifies data residency compliance by allowing vaults to be region-pinned. The combination of linearizable consistency and revision tokens ensures that access control decisions are deterministic and reproducible, which is essential for demonstrating to regulators that the system behaves as documented. Organizations adopting InferaDB do not need to build a separate compliance layer for authorization auditing — the compliance properties are inherent in the system's design.
