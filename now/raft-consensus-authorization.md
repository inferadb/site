Google's Zanzibar paper introduced a concept called the "new enemy problem" that remains the defining consistency challenge in authorization systems. The scenario is straightforward: Alice removes Bob's access to a document at 10:00:00. At 10:00:01, Bob's request hits a replica that has not yet received the revocation. The stale replica grants access. Bob sees the document. The permission was revoked, but the system served a stale grant because eventual consistency allowed a window where different replicas disagreed about the state of the world. In most distributed systems, this kind of temporary inconsistency is acceptable — a social media post appearing a few seconds late is not a security incident. In authorization, it is. A stale grant is a security violation, and no amount of convergence guarantees after the fact can undo the unauthorized access that already occurred.

InferaDB addresses this through Raft consensus, which provides linearizable writes across the cluster. Every write — creating a relationship tuple, modifying a type definition, updating a policy — flows through the Raft leader. The leader appends the entry to its log, replicates it to a quorum of followers via AppendEntries RPCs, and only commits the entry once a majority of nodes have durably stored it. After commit, the leader applies the entry to the state machine, which writes it to the B+ tree storage engine and appends a block to the vault's blockchain with a state root derived from the current Merkle tree. This means that once a write is acknowledged to the client, it is guaranteed to be durable and visible on any node that serves reads at or after that revision.

The write path is optimized for the authorization workload through adaptive batching. Individual writes are small — a single relationship tuple is typically under 200 bytes — but they arrive at high frequency during bulk operations like role migrations or organizational restructuring. The Raft leader accumulates pending entries and flushes them in batches, amortizing the cost of disk fsync and network round-trips across multiple entries. Write latency at p50 is 3 to 4 milliseconds, and p99 is 10 to 15 milliseconds. These numbers reflect the fundamental cost of distributed consensus — a network round-trip to a quorum plus a durable write — and cannot be meaningfully reduced without sacrificing the consistency guarantee. For authorization, this is the right trade-off: writes are less frequent than reads by orders of magnitude, and the cost of a stale grant far exceeds the cost of a few extra milliseconds on the write path.

Revision tokens are the mechanism that connects write consistency to read consistency. Every committed Raft entry increments a monotonically increasing revision counter. When a client performs a write, the response includes the revision token at which the write became visible. Clients can pass this token on subsequent reads to guarantee they observe at least that revision — a property called "read your writes" consistency. For clients that need the strongest guarantee, linearizable reads ensure that the response reflects the most recent committed state, which the leader can serve directly since it always holds the most up-to-date log. For clients that can tolerate bounded staleness — for example, when evaluating permissions for a batch of items where the relevant policies have not changed recently — follower reads with a revision floor provide lower latency by avoiding the round-trip to the leader.

Cross-region deployments introduce additional complexity. A Raft group spans nodes within a region for low-latency consensus, but organizations that operate across regions need causal ordering guarantees — if a permission is granted in one region, an action that depends on that permission in another region must observe the grant. InferaDB achieves this through causal consistency tokens that encode the revision state across regions. When a write in region A is acknowledged, the client receives a token that, when presented to region B, ensures that region B will not serve a response that is causally prior to the write. This allows cross-region authorization workflows to maintain correctness without requiring every read to contact every region, which would defeat the purpose of geographic distribution.
